Ayurveda is considered one of the world’s oldest holistic healing systems, developed in India over 3,000 years ago.
The word "Ayurveda" comes from Sanskrit: "Ayur" (life) and "Veda" (knowledge).
Ayurveda emphasizes the balance of mind, body, and spirit for overall health.
It classifies people into three primary doshas: Vata, Pitta, and Kapha.
Each person has a unique mix of the three doshas, called their "Prakriti."
Vata dosha represents air and ether elements, governing movement and creativity.
Pitta dosha represents fire and water elements, governing digestion and metabolism.
Kapha dosha represents water and earth elements, governing structure and stability.
Ayurveda teaches that imbalances in doshas lead to illness.
Ayurvedic treatment often includes diet, herbs, yoga, and meditation.
Agni, or digestive fire, is considered central to health.
Weak digestion is believed to produce Ama, a toxic buildup in the body.
Panchakarma is a cleansing therapy used to detoxify the body in Ayurveda.
Daily routine (Dinacharya) is considered vital for maintaining balance.
Seasonal routine (Ritucharya) helps adapt to changes in weather and environment.
Ayurveda recognizes six tastes: sweet, sour, salty, bitter, pungent, and astringent.
A balanced meal should ideally include all six tastes.
Turmeric is a widely used Ayurvedic herb with anti-inflammatory properties.
Ashwagandha is known as Indian ginseng and is used as an adaptogen.
Triphala is a classic Ayurvedic formula for digestion and detoxification.
Brahmi is an herb believed to enhance memory and concentration.
Neem is used in Ayurveda for its antibacterial and skin-healing properties.
Tulsi (Holy Basil) is considered a sacred plant with stress-reducing benefits.
Ghee (clarified butter) is highly valued in Ayurveda for its nourishing qualities.
Oil pulling (swishing oil in the mouth) is an Ayurvedic practice for oral health.
Abhyanga is the Ayurvedic practice of self-massage with warm oil.
Shirodhara involves pouring warm oil steadily on the forehead for relaxation.
Ayurveda emphasizes prevention over cure.
Meditation is seen as an important tool for calming the mind in Ayurveda.
Yoga is considered a complementary practice in Ayurveda.
Ayurveda states that health is harmony between body, senses, mind, and soul.
Ayurveda texts were first written in Sanskrit.
The main classical texts are Charaka Samhita, Sushruta Samhita, and Ashtanga Hridaya.
Charaka is known as the "Father of Medicine" in Ayurveda.
Sushruta is known as the "Father of Surgery" in Ayurveda.
Sushruta Samhita describes over 300 surgical procedures.
Ayurveda classifies disease into Nija (internal) and Agantuja (external) causes.
Sleep (Nidra), food (Ahara), and self-control (Brahmacharya) are the three pillars of health in Ayurveda.
Ayurveda recognizes the mind-body connection in disease development.
Ayurveda uses pulse diagnosis (Nadi Pariksha) to assess health.
Ayurveda is officially recognized by the World Health Organization (WHO).
India has a dedicated Ministry of AYUSH to promote Ayurveda.
Ayurveda influenced traditional medicine systems in Tibet, China, and Greece.
Ayurvedic texts describe more than 600 medicinal plants.
Ayurveda also includes mineral and animal-based preparations in treatments.
Rasayana therapy focuses on rejuvenation and anti-aging.
Ayurveda views the tongue as a map of the internal organs.
Drinking warm water is often recommended in Ayurveda to improve digestion.
Ayurveda encourages eating fresh, seasonal, and locally available foods.

Ayurveda considers taste as an indicator of how food affects the body and mind.
Spices like cumin, coriander, and fennel are commonly used for digestion in Ayurveda.
Ayurveda often recommends rising before sunrise to align with nature’s rhythms.
Sound sleep is considered essential for maintaining dosha balance.
Ayurveda suggests eating the heaviest meal at midday when digestion is strongest.
Copper water vessels are traditionally used in Ayurveda for their health benefits.
Ayurveda describes Ojas as the essence of immunity and vitality.
Ayurveda recognizes Prana, the vital life force, as essential for health.
Certain breathing techniques (Pranayama) are prescribed to balance doshas.
Ayurveda emphasizes mindful eating without distractions.

Ayurveda classifies body types based on physical, mental, and emotional traits.
Pregnancy care in Ayurveda emphasizes nourishing both mother and fetus with herbs and diet.
